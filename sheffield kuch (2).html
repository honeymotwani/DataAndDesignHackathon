Question 1 · Housing Archetypes

How can EPC data identify distinct housing archetypes in Sheffield?

We ran cluster analysis on Energy Performance Certificate data to group Sheffield's 187,000+ properties into five distinct archetypes — each with its own energy signature, construction era, tenure profile, and retrofit challenge. Understanding what types of homes exist is the foundation before asking where to intervene.

Why archetype-based thinking matters
Different home types need fundamentally different solutions — cavity wall insulation works for post-war semis but is impossible in solid-wall Victorian terraces requiring external or internal wall insulation instead
Targeting only geography misses structural patterns — a Pre-War Terrace in S7 and one in S14 share the same problem and can be served by the same supply chain
Archetype-based bulk commissioning reduces per-property retrofit cost by an estimated 30–40%, making programmes financially viable at scale
61% of Sheffield's stock was built before 1980 — one of England's most challenging cities for decarbonisation, meaning the Pre-War Terrace and Social Tower archetypes dominate the policy challenge

Question 2 · Energy Efficiency Need

Which areas show the greatest need for energy-efficiency upgrades?

We measure "need" across three dimensions: the efficiency gap (how far a home's current SAP score is from its achievable potential), the annual heating cost burden, and the concentration of D–G rated homes in each district. Together these pinpoint where upgrading delivers the biggest impact.

How to read these charts
Efficiency Gap = Potential SAP minus Current SAP, per EPC recommendations. A gap of 11 means the home could jump nearly two full letter grades if all measures were installed — e.g. from E to C.
Heating cost is EPC-modelled annual spend. High cost + low rating = likely fuel poverty, especially in low-income districts where the bills represent a larger share of household income.
The bubble chart overlays both signals: x-axis = heating cost, y-axis = efficiency gap, bubble size = % homes rated D or below. Districts in the top-right quadrant carry the most urgent combined burden.
The heatmap at the bottom lets you scan every district across five metrics simultaneously — darker warm cells = worse performance on that indicator.

Question 3 · Socio-Economic Priority

How can socio-economic data help prioritise where energy-efficiency support is most needed?

Energy need alone is insufficient for prioritisation. A wealthy area with inefficient homes can self-fund upgrades; a deprived area with the same inefficiency cannot. We overlay the Index of Multiple Deprivation (IMD) onto EPC metrics to produce a single composite Retrofit Priority Score that captures both energy need and social vulnerability.

How the priority score is calculated
Energy Need (40%) — % homes rated D–G, plus the average efficiency gap. Districts structurally incapable of retaining heat score highest here regardless of income level.
Financial Vulnerability (40%) — % social rented tenure plus average heating cost. Households least able to self-fund upgrades are given equal weight to energy need, ensuring social equity is embedded in the score.
Retrofit Potential (20%) — % pre-1980 stock and average number of outstanding EPC recommendations. Captures how much headroom for improvement exists before measures are exhausted.
All six features are normalised 0–1 using MinMax scaling before weighting, so no single metric dominates due to unit differences (£ vs % vs SAP points).

Question 4 · Time Series & EPC Forecasting

EPC trends 2012–2026 and retrofit trajectories forecast to 2030

Every EPC certificate carries a timestamp. By tracking ratings over 14 years we can measure the rate of improvement, detect slowdowns, and forecast whether Sheffield's current trajectory will reach government targets. We use Holt-Winters double exponential smoothing — a method that captures both level and trend in time series data without requiring a rigid linear assumption.

Understanding the forecasts
Holt-Winters smoothing weights recent observations more heavily than older ones (controlled by the alpha parameter). It also separately models the direction of change (beta), making it well-suited to policy-influenced data that shifts speed over time.
Dashed lines = forecasts. These are projections of current trends, not guarantees. They show what happens if no additional intervention occurs. The scenario chart below models what could happen with different levels of investment.
Red dashed = 70 SAP target. The UK Government requires social rented homes to reach Band C (SAP ≥ 70) by 2028, and all homes by 2035. Districts whose forecast lines do not reach 70 by then need accelerated programmes.
Use the year slider to animate the SAP chart and watch how the forecast updates as more observed data is included — illustrating how early years showed faster improvement that has since slowed.
Key Forecast Finding
At the current improvement rate of approximately 1.2 SAP points per year, Sheffield's average property will reach a score of 62.1 by 2030 — still 8 points short of the Band C threshold of 70. Districts S4, S9, and S36 would need improvement rates 2.5× faster than today to meet social housing targets by 2028. A full retrofit programme combining insulation, heat pumps, and solar PV could close this gap by 2029, but would require approximately £180–220 million of coordinated public and private investment across Sheffield.
